Creating a data strategy

National Australia Bank (NAB)  •  Investment Banking  •  Markets and Treasury Finance

The starting situation

  • No proper data strategy in place regarding the use of Business Intelligence, master data management, self-service analytics, etc.
  • Most of the internal reports (e.g. reconciliations spreadsheets, Profits and Loss, traders’ sign-off, etc.) were produced in Excel with extensive use of VLOOKUP-like formulas, unnecessary manual interventions and outdated VBA macros (no expertise in coding/development).
  • Some spreadsheets were also converted into PDFs and PPTs, annihilating any sort of data analysis.
  • There were no centralised way of disseminating data throughout the finance department. People were relying on SharePoint drives and were receiving 100+ emails per day.
  • Most of the reports were sent on a daily basis, with CSV or XLSX files attached. Some were inaccurate and only contained screenshots of Excel tables.
  • The email was used as a one-size-fits-all solution.

Solution

  • I collaborated with various departments (finance, IT) to identify all the stakeholders involved and the required/existing data sources.
  • I requested the implementation of a Power BI Server, so that we could streamline and automate reports. It would become the main point of entry/portal for all finance reportings.
  • In some cases, dedicated workflows had to be developed in Python (prototyping using Jupyter Notebook) before generating proper visualisations in Power BI.
  • Strengthening and enhancing already existing APIs.
  • Data governance also had to be enforced. Some of those reports were sent out to a large audience, which did not necessarily need access to it (leveraging groups in SailPoint).
  • Emails should be used to disseminate relevant and important information. It should not be used as a log monitoring tool.

Outcome

  • Creation of customised, state-of-the-art dashboards (e.g. Profit and Loss dashboard with drilling capabilities, period-over-period analysis, self-service analytics) for each and every team.
  • Implementation of fully automated dashboards (sourcing data from data lakes, data warehouses, APIs, etc. rather than manual mappings stored on shared drives).
  • Improved data governance: new SailPoint groups (Identity management tool) to limit access to certain Power BI folders and dashboards.
  • Development of data completeness dashboard (identifying when reports are ready for consumption, identifying bottlenecks in the data sourcing, etc.).
  • Monitoring usage metrics in workspaces (identifying popular dashboards, features, etc.).
  • The CFO, executive general managers and finance partners can now attend a meeting with a dynamic tool allowing them to deliver storytelling. They can answer questions on-the-fly by drilling into the visualisations.

Takeaways

  • Change management skills required: it takes time for people to adapt to new ways of doing things (even though the new tools may make a lot of sense on the paper)
  • Upskilling/training sessions were required so that end-users could fully embrace the new BI solutions.
